a blessing in disguise :: something that seems bad at first but turns out to be good :: Losing that job was a blessing in disguise — I found a better one.
a dime a dozen :: very common and easy to find, therefore not valuable :: Apps like this are a dime a dozen these days.
a piece of cake :: extremely easy to do :: The exam was a piece of cake compared to the practice tests.
add insult to injury :: to make a bad situation even worse :: To add insult to injury, the car broke down right after the speeding ticket.
back to the drawing board :: time to start over because the previous plan failed :: The proposal was rejected, so it's back to the drawing board.
ball is in your court :: it's your turn to make a decision or take action :: I've made my offer — the ball is in your court now.
barking up the wrong tree :: pursuing a mistaken or misguided line of thought :: If you think I broke it, you're barking up the wrong tree.
beat around the bush :: to avoid saying what you really mean :: Stop beating around the bush and tell me what happened.
bite off more than you can chew :: to take on more than you can handle :: She bit off more than she could chew by signing up for three classes.
bite the bullet :: to force yourself to do something unpleasant :: I'll just have to bite the bullet and go to the dentist.
break a leg :: good luck (typically said to performers) :: Break a leg out there tonight!
burn bridges :: to permanently ruin a relationship or opportunity :: Be careful not to burn bridges when you leave the company.
burn the midnight oil :: to work late into the night :: I had to burn the midnight oil to finish the report.
by the skin of your teeth :: just barely; only by the narrowest margin :: We caught the train by the skin of our teeth.
call it a day :: to stop working for the day :: It's getting late — let's call it a day.
cold feet :: nervousness or hesitation before a big decision :: He got cold feet right before the wedding.
costs an arm and a leg :: extremely expensive :: That new car costs an arm and a leg.
cross that bridge when you come to it :: deal with a problem only when it arises :: We'll cross that bridge when we come to it — no point worrying now.
cry over spilled milk :: to be upset about something that can't be changed :: It's done, so there's no use crying over spilled milk.
cut corners :: to do something quickly and cheaply, often poorly :: They cut corners on the construction, and now the roof leaks.
cut to the chase :: to skip the preliminaries and get to the point :: Let me cut to the chase — we can't afford it.
devil's advocate :: someone who argues an opposing view just for the sake of debate :: I'll play devil's advocate here — what if we're wrong?
don't judge a book by its cover :: don't decide based on appearances alone :: He looks rough, but don't judge a book by its cover — he's brilliant.
down to the wire :: until the very last possible moment :: The game went down to the wire before they finally scored.
drop in the bucket :: a very small amount compared to what's needed :: My donation is a drop in the bucket given the scale of the crisis.
elephant in the room :: an obvious problem nobody wants to discuss :: Are we going to talk about the elephant in the room?
every cloud has a silver lining :: every bad situation has some positive aspect :: I lost my job, but every cloud has a silver lining — now I can travel.
face the music :: to accept the unpleasant consequences of your actions :: He'll have to face the music when his parents find out.
feel under the weather :: to feel slightly ill :: I'm feeling under the weather, so I'll skip the meeting.
fish out of water :: someone uncomfortable in an unfamiliar situation :: I felt like a fish out of water at the fancy gala.
get your act together :: to organize yourself and improve your behavior :: You need to get your act together before the deadline.
give the benefit of the doubt :: to believe someone's account in the absence of contrary evidence :: I'll give him the benefit of the doubt this time.
go the extra mile :: to make more effort than is strictly necessary :: She always goes the extra mile for her clients.
hit the nail on the head :: to describe something exactly right :: You hit the nail on the head — that's the real problem.
hit the sack :: to go to bed :: I'm exhausted; I'm going to hit the sack.
hold your horses :: wait; slow down :: Hold your horses — I'm not done explaining yet.
in hot water :: in serious trouble :: He's in hot water with the boss after that mistake.
it's not rocket science :: it's not very difficult :: Just follow the recipe — it's not rocket science.
jump on the bandwagon :: to join a popular trend or activity :: Everyone jumped on the bandwagon once the team started winning.
keep your chin up :: stay positive in a difficult situation :: I know it's hard, but keep your chin up.
kick the bucket :: to die (informal, often humorous) :: He kicked the bucket at the ripe old age of ninety-five.
let the cat out of the bag :: to reveal a secret, often accidentally :: I let the cat out of the bag about the surprise party.
miss the boat :: to lose an opportunity by acting too late :: If we don't invest now, we'll miss the boat.
once in a blue moon :: very rarely :: We only see each other once in a blue moon these days.
on cloud nine :: extremely happy :: She's been on cloud nine since the promotion.
on the ball :: alert, competent, paying attention :: Our new assistant is really on the ball.
on the fence :: undecided between two options :: I'm on the fence about whether to take the job.
out of the blue :: completely unexpected :: She called me out of the blue after ten years.
piece of mind :: a small amount of one's frank opinion (often critical) :: I gave him a piece of my mind after he was late again.
pull someone's leg :: to tease or joke with someone :: Don't worry — I was just pulling your leg.
put all your eggs in one basket :: to risk everything on a single venture :: Diversify your investments — don't put all your eggs in one basket.
rain on someone's parade :: to spoil someone's enjoyment or plans :: I hate to rain on your parade, but the tickets are sold out.
read between the lines :: to infer meaning that isn't stated directly :: Read between the lines — she's clearly upset with you.
silver lining :: a positive aspect of an otherwise bad situation :: The silver lining of the delay was extra time to prepare.
spill the beans :: to reveal secret information :: Come on, spill the beans — what did he say?
steal someone's thunder :: to take credit or attention away from someone else :: Don't announce your engagement at her wedding — you'll steal her thunder.
take with a grain of salt :: to view skeptically; don't fully believe :: Take his version of events with a grain of salt.
the best of both worlds :: a situation combining the advantages of two things :: Working from home gives you the best of both worlds.
the last straw :: the final problem that makes a situation intolerable :: His rudeness today was the last straw.
the tip of the iceberg :: a small visible part of a much larger problem :: That bug is just the tip of the iceberg in this codebase.
throw in the towel :: to quit or give up :: After three failed attempts, I threw in the towel.
through thick and thin :: in good times and bad :: She stood by me through thick and thin.
under your nose :: in an obvious, visible place that you fail to notice :: The keys were right under your nose the whole time.
walk on eggshells :: to behave very cautiously to avoid upsetting someone :: I've been walking on eggshells around her since the argument.
when pigs fly :: never; an event that will never happen :: He'll apologize when pigs fly.
your guess is as good as mine :: I don't know any more than you do :: Why did they cancel? Your guess is as good as mine.
